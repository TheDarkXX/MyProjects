# Zinc Supplementation for Sleep Quality and Insomnia: Randomized Trial and Systematic Review Evidence

## Quick Reference

Zinc plausibly affects sleep through **zinc status → neurotransmitter modulation and melatonin-related pathways → sleep timing, sleep continuity, and perceived sleep quality**, but human evidence is **suggestive rather than definitive** because trials are small, heterogeneous, and often confounded by co-supplementation with melatonin and magnesium.[^1][^2]  

My concrete interpretation is that zinc is **reasonable as a deficiency-correction strategy or adjunct in selected groups**, especially older adults or shift-work populations with poor sleep quality, but it is **not yet an evidence-based stand-alone insomnia treatment** for zinc-replete adults.[^1][^3][^4]

***

## Evidence Base: What the Current Human Literature Actually Shows

The strongest zinc-specific synthesis available from the provided evidence is a 2024 systematic review by Shahraki Jazinaki et al. in *Health Science Reports*, which included **8 randomized controlled trials** of zinc supplementation for sleep outcomes in humans and found that most studies favored improved adult sleep quality, while effects on sleep disorders and nonadult sleep duration were inconsistent.[^1]  

The review included interventions lasting **4 to 48 weeks** and zinc doses ranging from **10 to 73.3 mg/day**, which creates major clinical heterogeneity and limits dose-response conclusions.[^1]  

The authors concluded that zinc “may” improve sleep quality, but they explicitly called for more clinical trials that account for baseline dietary zinc intake, zinc deficiency status, and recommended dietary allowance thresholds.[^1]  

Because the review was a **systematic review rather than a meta-analysis**, the evidence should be interpreted as directionally positive but not quantitatively pooled or definitive.[^1]  

The review also reported no author-declared conflict of interest, which lowers but does not eliminate concern about sponsorship bias.[^1]  

***

## Mechanistic Rationale: Melatonin, Neurotransmission, and Sleep Architecture

Zinc is an essential trace element involved in neurological function, and reviews describe possible sleep-promoting effects through neurotransmitter systems and melatonin-related regulation.[^5][^6]  

The mechanistic hypothesis is that zinc may support endogenous melatonin synthesis or metabolism, modulate excitatory and inhibitory signaling, and thereby influence sleep latency, continuity, and subjective quality.[^5][^7]  

This mechanism is biologically plausible but clinically underproven because mechanistic plausibility does not establish that zinc supplementation improves insomnia in zinc-replete humans.[^1][^4]  

Commercial and wellness summaries often present zinc as a sleep aid, but several also acknowledge that the most cited positive trial combined zinc with melatonin and magnesium, making zinc’s independent contribution uncertain.[^8][^9]  

Animal evidence supports zinc as a sleep modulator, including work by Cherasse and Urade describing dietary zinc effects on sleep biology, but animal data remain lower-level evidence for human insomnia decisions.[^10]

## Main Evidence Sections

### Zinc Alone for Sleep Quality

#### Study 1 — Older adults zinc-only randomized clinical trial by Afzali et al. (2021, *AFMN Biomedicine*)

A parallel randomized clinical trial by Afzali et al. in **150 older adults** assigned participants to **30 mg/day zinc for 70 days** or no supplement and measured sleep quality using the Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index and serum zinc before and after intervention.[^3]  

This trial reported that **73.3%** of the intervention group and **74.7%** of the control group had poor sleep quality at baseline, indicating comparable baseline sleep burden between groups.[^3]  

The zinc group had significant improvement in sleep quality compared with the control group after 70 days, and serum zinc also increased significantly in the intervention group compared with the control group.[^3]  

The trial is clinically relevant because it tested zinc alone in older adults, but it appears not to have used placebo blinding, which increases risk of expectancy and performance bias.[^3]  

**Evidence tag:** Afzali et al. (2021, *AFMN Biomedicine*) was a randomized clinical trial in **n=150 older adults**, **parallel-group**, **70 days**, apparently **open-label/no placebo**, using **30 mg/day zinc**.[^3]  

#### Study 2 — ICU nurses zinc-only randomized trial by Gholipour Baradari et al. (2018, *Workplace Health & Safety*)

A randomized controlled trial by Gholipour Baradari et al. in ICU nurses is repeatedly cited as a zinc-only human trial and is described as double-blind, randomized, and placebo-controlled.[^3][^8]  

The trial reportedly gave zinc supplementation to **60 ICU nurses** for **1 month** and found significant improvement in Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index scores compared with placebo.[^8]  

This study is important because ICU nurses are a high-stress, shift-work population with plausible sleep disruption and possible nutritional vulnerability.[^8]  

Generalizing this trial to the general adult population is limited because occupational stress, circadian disruption, and baseline sleep disruption may modify response to zinc.[^8]  

**Evidence tag:** Gholipour Baradari et al. (2018, *Workplace Health & Safety*) was an RCT in **n=60 ICU nurses**, reportedly **DB-PC**, **1 month**, zinc-only supplementation, with PSQI improvement.[^8]  

#### Study 3 — Systematic review by Shahraki Jazinaki et al. (2024, *Health Science Reports*)

A systematic review by Shahraki Jazinaki et al. included **8 RCTs** and found that most evidence suggested improved sleep quality in adults receiving zinc supplementation compared with control groups.[^1]  

The same review found zinc supplementation did **not** show a significant effect on “sleep disorders,” and findings for sleep duration in nonadults were contradictory.[^1]  

The review’s main limitation is that different populations, zinc doses, durations, and outcome tools prevented a clear pooled effect estimate.[^1]  

**Evidence tag:** Shahraki Jazinaki et al. (2024, *Health Science Reports*) was a **systematic review of randomized controlled trials**, **k=8 RCTs**, dose range **10–73.3 mg/day**, duration **4–48 weeks**, no declared COI.[^1]

### Zinc Combined With Melatonin and Magnesium

#### Study 4 — Rondanelli et al. combination trial (2011, *Journal of the American Geriatrics Society*)

A double-blind placebo-controlled clinical trial by Rondanelli et al. in **43 long-term care residents aged ≥70 years** with primary insomnia tested a nightly combination of melatonin, magnesium, and zinc for **8 weeks**.[^11][^12]  

The American Family Physician summary reports that the intervention contained **melatonin 5 mg, magnesium 225 mg, and zinc 11.25 mg** taken one hour before bedtime.[^11]  

The treatment group improved PSQI by **−7.1 points** from baseline compared with **−0.3 points** in the placebo group, with **P < .001**, which is statistically significant and likely clinically meaningful.[^11]  

A final PSQI score ≤5 occurred in **59%** of treated participants versus **14%** of placebo participants, with **P = .004**.[^11]  

The trial also reported better Leeds Sleep Evaluation Questionnaire domains, including getting to sleep, sleep quality, awakening, and morning alertness.[^11]  

The major interpretive problem is that zinc was not isolated, so the trial cannot determine whether the benefit came from melatonin, magnesium, zinc, their combination, or nonspecific effects.[^11][^12]  

**Evidence tag:** Rondanelli et al. (2011, *J Am Geriatr Soc*) was an RCT in **n=43 long-term care residents**, **DB-PC**, **8 weeks**, using **melatonin + magnesium + zinc**, with clinically large PSQI improvement.[^11][^12]  

#### Clinical Meaning of the Combination Trial

The Rondanelli trial supports a **complementary nutrient strategy** for older adults with primary insomnia, but it does not support the claim that zinc alone treats insomnia.[^11][^12]  

Melatonin has more direct evidence as a circadian and insomnia-related intervention than zinc, and an NCBI Bookshelf evidence update reported that systematic reviews and RCTs generally suggested favorable effects of melatonin on global and specific sleep outcomes in adults with insomnia.[^13]  

The best interpretation is that zinc may be part of a combined sleep-support formula, but melatonin is the more directly sleep-active component in the combination trial.[^11][^13]

### Magnesium Context: Why Co-Supplementation Matters

A 2024 randomized double-blind placebo-controlled crossover pilot trial by Breus et al. tested magnesium supplementation in **31 adults** with nonclinical insomnia symptoms and poor sleep quality.[^14]  

Participants received **1 g/day Upgraded Magnesium™** or placebo for **2 weeks**, followed by a 2-week washout and crossover to the alternative condition.[^14]  

The magnesium condition produced significant improvements compared with placebo across several sleep, mood, and activity outcomes, including sleep duration, deep sleep, sleep efficiency, readiness, activity balance, and heart-rate-variability readiness, with reported **p values < .05**.[^14]  

This matters because any trial combining zinc with magnesium cannot attribute sleep improvements to zinc unless the design includes separate zinc-only, magnesium-only, combination, and placebo arms.[^11][^14]  

A 2024 Nutrients trial listed in the magnesium literature examined zinc magnesium aspartate after partial sleep deprivation, further illustrating that zinc is often studied in combined formulations rather than isolated sleep protocols.[^15]

## Critical Nuance / Complementary Strategies

Zinc, magnesium, and melatonin should be framed as **complementary, not competing**, because they plausibly act through different but overlapping sleep-regulatory pathways.[^11][^13][^14]  

Melatonin is most defensible when the target is circadian timing or sleep-onset difficulty, while zinc is most defensible when low zinc intake, older age, malabsorption, vegan diet, or documented deficiency is present.[^1][^4][^13]  

For patients with chronic insomnia symptoms, cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia and evaluation for sleep apnea, restless legs syndrome, anxiety, depression, medication effects, and circadian rhythm disorders remain more evidence-based than empiric zinc supplementation.[^4][^13]  

Zinc supplementation should not be presented as a replacement for insomnia diagnosis or treatment because the systematic review evidence remains heterogeneous and does not establish a standardized therapeutic dose.[^1][^4]

## Toxicity / Safety / Drug Interactions

1. **Copper deficiency is the most important long-term safety issue**, because sustained high zinc intake can impair copper absorption and contribute to anemia or neurological symptoms.[^4][^9]  

2. **Gastrointestinal adverse effects are common practical barriers**, including nausea, abdominal discomfort, metallic taste, headache, and reduced appetite.[^4]  

3. **Dose ceilings matter**, because UK consumer guidance cited in the provided material advises not exceeding **25 mg/day from supplements without medical advice**, while North American consumer sources often cite **40 mg/day** as an adult tolerable upper intake.[^4][^9]  

4. **Older adults, people with malabsorption, dialysis patients, restrictive diets, and institutionalized populations may be more appropriate candidates for zinc assessment**, because deficiency correction is more plausible than benefit in zinc-replete adults.[^1][^3][^4]  

5. **Medication review is prudent before supplementation**, because minerals can interfere with absorption of some medications and may need spacing from drugs such as certain antibiotics or thyroid medications, although specific drug-interaction trial data were not provided in the supplied sources.[^4]  

6. **Long-term zinc above nutritional replacement doses should not be self-directed**, especially when sleep symptoms could reflect obstructive sleep apnea, restless legs syndrome, depression, anxiety, substance use, or medication adverse effects.[^4][^13]

## Overall Evidence Judgment

The current evidence supports the statement that zinc supplementation **can improve subjective sleep quality in some human populations**, particularly adults in small randomized trials and older adults with poor sleep quality.[^1][^3][^8]  

The evidence does **not** support zinc as a proven stand-alone treatment for chronic insomnia, because the best-known clinically large trial used zinc with melatonin and magnesium, and the 2024 systematic review found heterogeneous results without a pooled meta-analytic effect.[^1][^11]  

The most clinically defensible use is a **time-limited trial of modest-dose zinc when deficiency risk is present**, ideally after dietary assessment or serum zinc evaluation and with attention to copper balance.[^1][^3][^4]  

For presentations, the safest high-confidence wording is: **“Zinc supplementation shows promising but heterogeneous RCT evidence for improving subjective sleep quality, especially in adults with poor sleep or deficiency risk; it should be considered adjunctive and not a substitute for established insomnia evaluation or treatment.”**[^1][^4][^13]
