# L-Theanine Supplementation for Alpha Brain Waves,ation, Sleep Quality, and Anxiety Reduction

## Quick Reference

L-theanine is a tea-derived non-protein amino acid that appears to promote “relaxed alertness” through glutamate-related signaling, increased alpha-band EEG activity, and modulation of stress physiology → lower perceived stress/cortisol → improved relaxation and, in some trials, better sleep quality and attention without clear sedative impairment.[^1][^2][^3]  
My evidence-based opinion is that **200–400 mg/day L-theanine is a plausible adjunct for stress-related sleep disturbance and anxiety symptoms**, but it is **not yet a stand-alone insomnia or anxiety-disorder treatment**, because the best human evidence is small, heterogeneous, and often ingredient-industry funded.[^2][^4][^5]

***

## Alpha Brain Waves and Acute Relaxation

#### Study 1 — Acute AlphaWave® L-theanine stress trial (Evans et al., 2021, *Neurology and Therapy*)

A randomized, triple-blind, placebo-controlled crossover trial by Evans et al. in **16 moderately stressed healthy adults aged 19–60 years** tested a **single 200 mg dose** of AlphaWave® L-theanine against placebo with a mental arithmetic stressor and EEG, cortisol, blood pressure, heart rate, and safety outcomes (RCT, n=16, triple-blind PC crossover, single-dose, ⚠️ industry-linked ingredient study).[^2]  
The trial found that 200 mg L-theanine significantly increased **frontal-region alpha power** versus placebo during acute stress, which is physiologically consistent with relaxation without drowsiness.[^2]  
The same trial found that 200 mg L-theanine significantly reduced **salivary cortisol** versus placebo after the mental arithmetic stressor, supporting an acute stress-buffering effect.[^2]  
The study reported that the relaxation effect extended up to **3 hours post-dose**, which is longer than some earlier low-dose EEG studies using 50 mg L-theanine.[^2]  
No major safety signal was reported in this single-dose study, but the sample was too small to exclude uncommon adverse events.[^2]

#### Study 2 — Alpha-wave generation after oral L-theanine in early EEG work

The Evans et al. paper cites earlier human EEG work reporting that oral L-theanine generated alpha waves in occipital and parietal brain regions within about **40 minutes**, which supports biological plausibility for a rapid calming effect.[^2]  
A narrative review by Nobre et al. concluded that L-theanine can affect mental state through alpha activity and relaxation-related neurophysiology, but the review was brief and not a quantitative meta-analysis.[^6]  
A controlled EEG study by Gomez-Ramirez et al. evaluated L-theanine during a visuospatial attention task and reported modulation of **alpha-band oscillatory brain activity**, supporting a cognitive-neurophysiologic effect rather than simple sedation.[^7]

#### Study 3 — High-anxiety subgroup attention/reaction trial

A randomized placebo-controlled study by Higashiyama et al. in **18 university-aged participants** classified by anxiety propensity tested **200 mg L-theanine** and reported stronger alpha activity, lower heart rate, better visual attention, and improved reaction-time response in high-anxiety participants, while minimal-anxiety participants showed less clear benefit (RCT, n=18, PC crossover/acute testing, short duration).[^8]  
The clinical relevance of this trial is limited by the very small sample and subgroup structure, but it supports the hypothesis that L-theanine may work best in people with elevated stress or anxiety arousal.[^8]

### Interpretation: Alpha waves are a mechanism marker, not a clinical endpoint

Alpha-band increases are consistent with relaxed wakefulness, but alpha power itself is not a patient-important clinical endpoint unless paired with symptom improvement or functional benefit.[^2][^7]  
The most defensible claim is that **single-dose 200 mg L-theanine can acutely shift EEG and cortisol markers toward relaxation under laboratory stress**, not that it reliably treats clinical anxiety or insomnia after one dose.[^2][^8]

## Stress, Anxiety, and Mood Symptoms

#### Study 4 — Healthy adult stress-related symptoms trial (Hidese et al., 2019, *Nutrients*)

A randomized placebo-controlled trial by Hidese et al. in **30 healthy adults aged 48 years and older** tested **200 mg/day L-theanine for 4 weeks** and assessed stress-related symptoms, sleep, and cognition (RCT, n=30, PC, 4 weeks).[^9]  
The trial reported improvements in Self-Rating Depression Scale, State-Trait Anxiety Inventory-Trait, and Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index scores after L-theanine compared with placebo, suggesting benefit in stress-related symptoms and sleep quality.[^9]  
The same trial reported improvements in verbal fluency and executive-function measures, particularly letter fluency, indicating that relaxation did not come with measurable cognitive suppression in this population.[^9]  
The trial’s main limitation is small sample size, which increases risk of overestimating benefit and limits subgroup inference.[^9]

#### Study 5 — Schizophrenia and schizoaffective disorder augmentation trial

A double-blind randomized placebo-controlled two-center trial by Ritsner et al. in **60 patients with DSM-IV schizophrenia or schizoaffective disorder** tested **400 mg/day L-theanine added to antipsychotic treatment for 8 weeks** (RCT, n=60 randomized; n=40 completed, DB-PC, 8 weeks).[^4]  
Compared with placebo, L-theanine significantly reduced Hamilton Anxiety Rating Scale scores (**P = .015**) and PANSS positive (**P = .009**) and general psychopathology scores (**P < .001**) using the PANSS three-dimensional model.[^4]  
Using the PANSS five-dimensional model, L-theanine significantly reduced PANSS positive (**P = .004**) and activation-factor (**P = .006**) scores compared with placebo.[^4]  
Effect sizes were modest to moderate, with Cohen’s **d = 0.09–0.39**, which is statistically supportive but clinically limited.[^4]  
PANSS negative symptoms, CANTAB neurocognitive tasks, general functioning, side effects, and quality-of-life measures were not significantly improved.[^4]  
The study concluded that L-theanine augmentation was safe and well tolerated over 8 weeks, but completion was only **40/60 participants**, which weakens certainty.[^4]

#### Study 6 — Systematic review in mental disorders

A 2024 systematic review of randomized controlled trials by Sarris-type psychiatric supplementation literature identified **11 studies from six countries** covering schizophrenia, ADHD, OCD, major depressive disorder, sleep disorders, generalized anxiety disorder, and Tourette syndrome (Systematic review, k=11 studies).[^5]  
The review concluded that L-theanine reduced psychiatric symptoms more effectively than control conditions in schizophrenia, anxiety disorders, and ADHD, but emphasized that more studies are needed to validate findings and clarify mechanisms.[^5]  
The review reported no competing interests, which reduces but does not eliminate concern about selective publication or ingredient-study bias.[^5]  
This review is important because it synthesizes clinical populations, but it is not a meta-analysis with pooled effect sizes in the available abstracted information.[^5]

#### Study 7 — Major depressive disorder open-label trial

An open-label study by Hidese et al. in patients with major depressive disorder tested **250 mg/day L-theanine for 8 weeks** and reported improvements in depressive symptoms, anxiety, sleep disturbance, verbal memory, and executive function (Open-label, clinical MDD sample, 8 weeks).[^10]  
Because the study lacked placebo control and blinding, it is hypothesis-generating rather than confirmatory for anxiety or sleep outcomes.[^10]

### Interpretation: strongest anxiety signal is stress-linked, not diagnostic anxiety treatment

The strongest anxiety-related evidence supports L-theanine as an adjunct for **stress arousal and anxiety symptoms**, especially in moderately stressed or psychiatrically treated populations.[^4][^5][^9]  
The evidence is insufficient to claim that L-theanine is an evidence-based monotherapy for generalized anxiety disorder, panic disorder, OCD, or major depression.[^5][^10]

## Sleep Quality and Sleep Architecture

#### Study 8 — 2025 systematic review of L-theanine sleep trials

A 2025 systematic review of dietary supplementation trials identified **13 eligible human trials with n=550 total participants**, including **11 randomized controlled trials** and **2 single-arm open-label trials**, testing **50–900 mg/day** standalone L-theanine for sleep-related outcomes (Systematic review, k=13 trials, n=550).[^11]  
The review concluded that **200–450 mg/day** appears safe and effective for supporting healthy sleep in adults, with benefits reported for sleep latency, maintenance, efficiency, sleep satisfaction, and refreshment on waking.[^11]  
The review emphasized the need for higher-quality trials using objective sleep measures and for studies in people with clinical insomnia.[^11]  
This is currently the most directly relevant synthesis for sleep, but the lack of pooled effect sizes in the available abstract limits certainty about magnitude and clinical meaningfulness.[^11]

#### Study 9 — 28-day AlphaWave® L-theanine trial

A randomized double-blind placebo-controlled trial by Moulin et al. in healthy adults with moderate stress tested AlphaWave® L-theanine for **28 days** and measured stress, sleep, mood, cognition, vital signs, clinical chemistry, hematology, and adverse events (RCT, n reported as 38 in sponsor material and n=30 enrolled in a trade summary, DB-PC, 28 days, ⚠️ industry-linked ingredient study).[^3][^12]  
The peer-reviewed PMC report states that AlphaWave® L-theanine significantly decreased perceived stress from baseline and improved sleep-related outcomes, including a significant decrease in light sleep at days 14 and 28 versus placebo.[^3]  
The same report states that cognitive attention improved over the 28-day study period and that the supplement was safe and well tolerated.[^3]  
One reported sleep/cognition-related comparison reached **p = 0.005**, but the excerpted information does not specify the exact outcome tied to that p-value, so it should not be overinterpreted.[^3]  
The authors explicitly called for larger and longer randomized controlled trials to reduce inter-individual variability and placebo-effect concerns.[^3]

#### Study 10 — Healthy older adult stress/sleep/cognition trial

The Hidese et al. 2019 RCT found that PSQI total score and subcomponents related to sleep latency, sleep disturbance, and use of sleep medication improved after **200 mg/day for 4 weeks**, which aligns with the 2025 sleep systematic review’s conclusion for stress-related sleep support.[^9][^11]  
The trial population was older healthy adults rather than patients with diagnosed insomnia, so the finding is best framed as sleep-quality support rather than insomnia treatment.[^9]

### Interpretation: sleep benefit is plausible but condition-specific

L-theanine’s sleep effect appears most plausible when sleep difficulty is driven by stress arousal, presleep cognitive activation, or poor perceived sleep quality.[^9][^11]  
The available evidence does not justify claims that L-theanine reliably increases total sleep time, treats obstructive sleep apnea, replaces cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia, or matches approved hypnotic medications.[^11]

## Cognition, Relaxed Attention, and Caffeine Combination Evidence

#### Study 11 — L-theanine plus caffeine acute cognition trial

A randomized controlled study by Haskell et al. tested L-theanine, caffeine, and their combination and found that the combination improved aspects of cognition and mood more consistently than either compound alone (RCT, acute crossover design).[^13]  
This study is relevant because many people consume L-theanine naturally through tea, where it coexists with caffeine and may modify caffeine’s alerting effects.[^13]

#### Study 12 — Sustained attention with caffeine plus L-theanine

A randomized controlled study by Foxe et al. tested **100 mg L-theanine plus 50 mg caffeine** during sustained attention and found reduced errors during monotonous task performance compared with placebo (RCT, acute cognitive testing).[^14]  
This supports the “calm focus” concept, but it does not isolate L-theanine because caffeine was co-administered.[^14]

#### Study 13 — Middle-aged and older cognitive function trial

A randomized placebo-controlled study by Baba et al. evaluated L-theanine in middle-aged and older adults and reported improvements in attention, working memory, and executive-function-related measures (RCT, placebo-controlled, 12 weeks).[^15]  
This trial suggests possible cognitive benefit beyond acute stress relief, but cognition outcomes should not be conflated with clinical anxiety or insomnia outcomes.[^15]

### Interpretation: cognitive preservation supports non-sedating relaxation

The cognitive literature supports the claim that L-theanine can promote relaxation without obvious acute cognitive impairment in studied populations.[^7][^13][^14]  
The strongest cognitive effects may occur when L-theanine is paired with caffeine, which is mechanistically and clinically distinct from bedtime L-theanine use.[^13][^14]

## Critical Nuance / Complementary Strategies

L-theanine should be framed as **complementary, not competing**, with sleep hygiene, cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia, stress-management practices, exercise, and clinically indicated pharmacotherapy.[^5][^11]  
For stress-related insomnia, a rational approach is to use L-theanine as an adjunct that targets presleep arousal while continuing behavioral sleep interventions that have stronger clinical-outcome evidence.[^11]  
For anxiety disorders or psychiatric illness, L-theanine should not replace psychotherapy, antidepressants, antipsychotics, or other prescribed care, because the psychiatric evidence is adjunctive and heterogeneous.[^4][^5]  
For daytime stress, 200 mg appears supported for acute physiological relaxation, while bedtime use is more defensible when the target is perceived sleep quality rather than pharmacologic sedation.[^2][^9][^11]  
For performance or alertness, L-theanine plus caffeine has separate evidence for attention and vigilance, but that combination may be inappropriate near bedtime because caffeine can worsen sleep.[^13][^14]

## Toxicity / Safety / Drug Interactions

1. **Sedative, hypnotic, anxiolytic, and antipsychotic co-use requires caution**, because L-theanine has central nervous system activity and has been studied as adjunctive therapy in psychiatric populations rather than as an unsupervised replacement.[^4][^5]  
2. **Antihypertensive therapy and low baseline blood pressure warrant caution**, because stress studies report effects on heart rate, blood pressure physiology, or stress-induced cardiovascular responses.[^2][^16]  
3. **Pregnancy and lactation should be treated as insufficient-evidence contexts**, because the cited adult RCTs and systematic reviews do not establish reproductive safety.[^5][^11]  
4. **Children and adolescents require clinician oversight**, because most higher-quality sleep, stress, and EEG studies are in adults or small specialized groups.[^5][^11]  
5. **People with bipolar disorder, psychosis, OCD, major depression, ADHD, or generalized anxiety disorder should avoid self-directed medication changes**, because psychiatric trials are small, heterogeneous, and generally adjunctive.[^4][^5][^10]  
6. **Product quality matters**, because branded ingredient trials may not generalize to all supplements with variable purity, dose accuracy, or contaminants.[^2][^3]  
7. **Common trial doses range widely from 50–900 mg/day**, but the most practical evidence-supported range for adult stress-sleep support is **200–450 mg/day**.[^11]  
8. **Adverse events appear uncommon in short trials**, but small samples and short durations limit detection of rare or delayed harms.[^2][^3][^11]

***

## Overall Evidence-Based Conclusion

The strongest evidence supports L-theanine as a **short-term, low-risk adjunct** for stress-related relaxation and sleep-quality support, with the most consistent practical dose range around **200–400 mg/day** and acute EEG/cortisol effects after **200 mg**.[^2][^3][^9][^11]  
The most concrete mechanistic claim is that L-theanine can increase alpha-band activity and reduce stress physiology under acute challenge, while the most concrete clinical claim is that it may improve perceived stress and sleep quality in adults with moderate stress or stress-related symptoms.[^2][^3][^9][^11]  
The evidence is not strong enough to classify L-theanine as a proven treatment for clinical insomnia, generalized anxiety disorder, depression, OCD, ADHD, or schizophrenia, although adjunctive psychiatric signals are promising and justify larger independent RCTs.[^4][^5][^10][^11]  
My practical conclusion is that L-theanine is **reasonable to consider for adults with mild-to-moderate stress-related sleep difficulty who want a non-sedating supplement**, but presentations should avoid disease-treatment language and disclose that several positive branded-ingredient studies are small and likely commercially linked.[^2][^3][^11]
